A Code of the Individual Organism

Philipp Bello

Consolidated English edition · Document IV & Session On Goods (V2) integrated

A psychological-normative autobiography of the individual organism, cast in the form of a code. The Spanish original, Código de Vida, remains the source of record; this is its consolidated English edition. Dispositions are given verbatim; working notes, cross-references and doctrinal apparatus are omitted from each article and gathered separately.

Each disposition carries its species — descriptive predictive interpretive prescriptive — and, where applicable, a status marker: hypothetical amended skeleton reserved recension pending.

Preliminary Title

principles, method, rules of reading

§ I — On the Fundamental Principle

TP 01 The individual organism resists descriptive

The individual organism resists. So long as it exists, this disposition is true; its truth does not depend on the degree of activity or passivity with which the resistance is exercised, nor on whether the individual organism is aware of it.

§ II — On the Species of Disposition and their Method

TP 02 The four species of disposition interpretive

The dispositions of this Code are of four species: descriptive, predictive, interpretive normative, and prescriptive normative.

TP 03 Descriptive dispositions interpretive

Descriptive dispositions are those that ascertain causes and their present or past effects. They refer to what the individual organism is or has been. Their value is one of truth or of falsity.

TP 04 Predictive dispositions interpretive

Predictive dispositions are those that, from causes ascertained as constant, anticipate future effects. They refer to what the individual organism will do or will undergo under certain conditions. Their value is one of probability.

TP 05 Efficient or final causes interpretive

The causes with which descriptive and predictive dispositions deal may be efficient or final.

TP 06 Interpretive normatives interpretive

Interpretive normatives are those that govern the reading of this Code. They bind whoever interprets it. Their value is one of validity.

TP 07 Prescriptive normatives interpretive

Prescriptive normatives are those that establish rules of conduct which the individual organism adopts. According to their content, they command, prohibit, or permit. Their value is one of validity; their efficacy is contingent.

TP 08 Criteria for dividing the species interpretive

The division between descriptive and predictive dispositions is made by temporal index. The division between interpretive and prescriptive normatives is made by addressee. No criterion is common to both divisions.

TP 09 The species is determined by content interpretive

The species of each disposition is determined by its content and not by its location. One and the same Book, Title, Chapter, or article may contain dispositions of all four species.

TP 10 Superposition of species interpretive

One and the same disposition may be of two superposed species. Superposition does not entail the derivation of one species from the other.

TP 11 Every prescriptive is founded on constatation interpretive

Every prescriptive disposition is founded on a descriptive one, on a predictive one, or on both. None is adopted in the abstract. Interpretive dispositions are founded on no constatation whatsoever.

TP 12 Distance between norm and practice interpretive

The distance between norm and practice is predicated solely of prescriptive dispositions. The habitual behavior of the individual organism does not conform perfectly to the prescriptive disposition, nor is the prescriptive disposition derived mechanically from behavior. This distance invalidates neither the norm nor the practice: it constitutes the very space in which this Code operates.

TP 13 Predominance of species by Book interpretive amended

Descriptive dispositions predominate in Book One, where they constitute the ethology of the individual organism. Predictive ones predominate in Book Two, where they constitute its morphology. The remaining Books contain dispositions of all four species as the subject matter requires.

TP 14 Qualitative method and quantitative complement interpretive

The method of this Code is eminently qualitative, as befits the juridical tradition from which its technique derives. The dispositions specify their objects by means of characters, degrees, modalities, and other determinations not reducible to magnitude.

§ III — On Finality

TP 15 Ends arranged in a series descriptive

The individual organism is ordered to ends arranged in a series: it grows in order to gain, and it gains so that its life may hold worth as form.

TP 16 Determination descends from the ultimate end descriptive

In every series of ends the determination descends: the proximate end receives its content from the ultimate end. No intermediate term is determined by itself alone.

TP 17 The ultimate end is not knowable from within the series descriptive

The ultimate end is not knowable by the one who travels the series. Its oblique perception, from within the series, manifests itself as a surplus of meaning over the cause.

TP 18 Growth is the integration of resistance descriptive

Growth consists in the integration of resistance. Desire opens it, habit consolidates it, memory preserves it as rule.

TP 19 Function of the Code, to read one's own position interpretive

This Code exists so that the individual organism, finding itself impeded from perceiving its own position, may read the one it occupied when it was still able to perceive it.

TP 20 The Code describes the conditions of the act interpretive

This Code describes the conditions of the act. It does not describe the act.

§ III bis — On the Terminal Term of the Series of Ends

TP 44 The series of ends terminates (interpretive) interpretive

"The series of ends to which the individual organism is ordered terminates. This disposition is interpretive: its value is validity and not truth, and it is grounded in no finding of fact."

TP 45 The terminal term is not knowable from within interpretive

"The terminal term of the series is not knowable by one who traverses it. The end named in § III — that the life count as form — is the last term visible from within the series, and not its terminal term."

TP 46 Nothing is predicated of the terminal term interpretive

"Nothing is predicated of the terminal term. This Code neither affirms nor denies its existence, and the disposition of the first article of this paragraph does not constitute an affirmation of existence, being of the interpretive species."

TP 47 Impredicability proceeds from position, not insufficiency interpretive

"The impredicability of the terminal term does not proceed from any insufficiency in the individual organism's knowledge, but from the position the term occupies with respect to the series. It is that to which the series is subordinated, and not that of which the series may speak."

TP 48 Prohibition on predicating nature, origin, or identity interpretive

"The interpreter is forbidden to predicate of the terminal term any nature, origin, or identity. The proposition that reduces it to something else — to a force of the individual organism, to a figure of its history, to a form of the social — is a predication, and is barred to the interpreter for the same reason as the proposition that posits it."

TP 49 Prohibition on converting the interpretive disposition into descriptive interpretive

"The interpreter is forbidden to convert the interpretive disposition of this paragraph into a descriptive disposition. The conversion destroys the normative structure of this Code."

§ IV — On Structures

TP 21 Ternary, binary and serial structures interpretive

The dispositions of this Code describe ternary, binary, and serial structures. The distinction is one of interpretation and governs the reading of all the Books.

TP 22 Ternary structures interpretive

Ternary are the structures of three coordinate terms that reciprocally limit one another on a single plane. None subsists without the others. Their relation is one of equilibrium.

TP 23 Predominance of one ternary term predictive

The predominance of one ternary term over the other two produces, foreseeably, the consumption, the fossilization, or the emptying of the structure, according to which of them predominates.

TP 24 Binary structures interpretive

Binary are the structures of two terms, of which one is actual and the other latent at each moment. Latency is not absence, but availability.

TP 25 The two classes of binary structure interpretive

Binary structures are of two classes. Some oppose being to the ought; in them the latent term is invoked. Others oppose what appears to what is; in them the latent term is noticed.

TP 26 Irreversible polarity of the binary interpretive

The polarity of the binary structure is irreversible. Only one of the terms admits of being invoked or noticed; the other imposes itself by itself.

TP 27 Serial structures interpretive

Serial are the structures whose terms are ordered by a relation of service or of foundation, each referring to the next, up to a terminal term that refers to no other. Their terms neither equilibrate nor oscillate: they subordinate.

TP 28 A taxonomy is not a ternary structure interpretive

The enunciation of three terms does not by itself constitute a ternary structure. A taxonomy divides; a ternary structure equilibrates.

TP 29 The third term that does not equilibrate interpretive

A structure does not become ternary through the appearance of a third term, if this term suspends, founds, negates, or observes the entire structure instead of limiting its terms on the same plane.

TP 30 Presumption of ternarity interpretive

In doubt as to the qualification of a structure, it is presumed ternary. Whoever asserts oscillation or subordination must show that the remaining term operates on a distinct plane.

TP 31 Scope of the doctrine of structures interpretive

What is provided in this paragraph is predicated of the structures of the individual organism and of its relations. It is not predicated of the taxonomic divisions of this Code, which are of method and not of ontology.

§ IV bis — On Traits and States

TP 50 A trait is found, a state comes about descriptive

"A trait is found. A state comes about."

TP 51 Potestative, casual and mixed states descriptive

"States are distinguished according to what their coming about depends upon. A state is potestative where it depends upon an act of the individual organism; casual, where it depends upon forces foreign to its will; mixed, where it depends upon an act of the individual organism and, further, upon forces foreign to its will."

TP 52 No state comes about by the mere will descriptive

"No state comes about by the mere will of the individual organism. A potestative state depends upon an act, not upon a decision."

TP 53 The prescriptive bears upon the act prescriptive

"A prescriptive disposition bears upon the act. It does not bear upon the trait, nor upon the casual state, nor upon the casual element of the mixed state."

TP 54 The coming about of a mixed state is predictive matter predictive

"The coming about of a mixed state is the matter of a predictive disposition. No disposition of this Code assures it, and sustained practice does not secure it."

TP 55 A disposition commanding what does not depend upon the act is held not written interpretive

"A disposition that commands what does not depend upon the act is held not to have been written as to what it commands, and subsists, if it can be induced from its text, as the corresponding predictive disposition."

§ V — On the Value of Sources and Hypothetical Dispositions

TP 32 Self-observation and the deficiency of memory descriptive

The descriptive and predictive dispositions of this Code are founded on the self-observation of the individual organism. Self-observation depends on memory, and the memory of the individual organism presents constitutive deficiencies.

TP 33 Hypothetical character of the recollection of inner states interpretive

Every disposition founded predominantly on the recollection of inner states is of hypothetical character until it is verified by means independent of the memory of the individual organism.

TP 34 Independent means of verification interpretive

Independent means of verification include, among others: the written record contemporaneous with the observed state; the observation of qualified third parties; clinical measurement; the systematic comparison of periods under controlled conditions; and any other means that does not depend exclusively on retrospective recollection.

TP 35 The classification as hypothetical does not exclude from the Code interpretive

The classification of a disposition as hypothetical does not preclude its inclusion in this Code nor reduce its usefulness. It signifies only that its value of truth or of probability remains provisional, and that the individual organism has the duty to seek its verification when the means are available.

TP 36 Precedence is not causation interpretive

Precedence is not causation. No disposition of Book One authorizes reading an anterior state as the cause of a posterior state by the mere fact of having preceded it.

§ V bis — On the Proof of Descriptive and Predictive Dispositions

TP 57 The isolated fact is recorded as a state interpretive

A fact evidenced only once in the archive constitutes a state and is recorded as such. Its future repetition may not be affirmed or denied by predictive disposition, for want of inductive basis. Its non-repetition may be ordered by normative disposition. A resolution adopted with respect to an isolated fact is recorded as a mandate, not as a forecast.

§ VI — On Interpretation

TP 37 The dispositions are interpreted by one another interpretive

The dispositions of this Code are interpreted by one another, with attention to the species of each. A descriptive disposition does not derogate a prescriptive one. A prescriptive disposition does not falsify a descriptive one. Between dispositions of different species there is no contradiction, but distance.

TP 38 The complementary notes are not part of the Code interpretive

The complementary notes do not form part of this Code. They serve to verify the descriptive and predictive dispositions, and do not bind whoever interprets it.

TP 39 The doctrinal writings do not bind interpretive

The doctrinal writings that ground the dispositions of this Code do not form part of it nor bind whoever interprets it.

TP 56 A disposition does not change species by the will of the interpreter interpretive

"A disposition does not change species by the will of the interpreter. Species is determined by content, in accordance with § II, and the conversion of one species into another is a defect of interpretation."

§ VII — On Validity and Reform

TP 40 Validity and express reform interpretive

This Code is in force from its drafting. It is reformed only expressly. Contrary practice does not derogate it.

TP 41 Correction and derogation by species interpretive

A descriptive disposition belied by the facts is corrected. A predictive one belied is corrected or derogated. A prescriptive one unfulfilled is not derogated by its non-fulfillment.

TP 42 Lapse of transitional provisions interpretive

Transitional provisions lapse upon the fulfillment of their term or upon the extinction of the situation they regulate, without need of express derogation.

TP 43 Once the organism is extinguished, the Code subsists as text descriptive

Once the individual organism is extinguished, this Code ceases to be in force and subsists as text.

Book One — On the Individual Organism

ethology

Title I — On the Constitution of the Individual Organism

Chapter I — On Unity and its Organs

L1 01 The limit is not anatomical but the site of resistance descriptive

The individual organism coincides neither with its body nor with its mind. Its limit is not anatomical: it is the site where its resistance is exercised.

L1 02 Body and mind as particular wills descriptive

What presents itself as an undifferentiated unity contains organs that oppose it with a resistance of their own. The body and the mind of the individual organism are, with respect to it, distinct particular wills.

L1 03 Integration into an organism of higher order descriptive

The individual organism may integrate with what is external and thereby constitute an organism of higher order. Integration does not suppress the plurality of the integrated wills.

Chapter II — On the Neurological Condition

L1 04 The neurological condition is misalignment, not incapacity descriptive

The neurological condition of the individual organism does not constitute an incapacity. It constitutes a misalignment between the complexity of the functions it performs and the conditions under which it performs them. The difference between incapacity and misalignment is one of nature and not of degree: incapacity supposes an absolute limit; misalignment, a discordance susceptible of management.

L1 05 The relationship with treatment as constitutive datum descriptive

The prolonged relationship with treatment is not a biographical accident but a constitutive datum, as much as professional formation or family history.

L1 06 Dispositions presupposing clinical qualifications (hypothetical) interpretivehypothetical

The dispositions of this Code that presuppose clinical qualifications not yet concluded have a hypothetical character and are interpreted as working hypotheses.

Chapter III — On Intelligence as Attribute and as Risk

L1 07 Intelligence as resource and as risk factor descriptive

The intellectual capacity of the individual organism operates simultaneously as a resource and as a risk factor. It is a resource insofar as it allows it to understand its conditions, to formulate strategies and to perform complex functions. It is a risk insofar as it allows it to construct elaborate justifications for compulsive conduct and to confuse affective thought with rational thought.

L1 08 Sophistication is not evidence of rationality descriptive

The sophistication of a justification is not evidence of its rationality, but of the intensity of the compulsion that sustains it.

L1 09 Inversion of the presumption of rationality interpretive

With respect to affectively charged contents, the presumption of rationality is inverted: the default hypothesis is that recurrent thought is rationalized rumination. The presumption admits proof to the contrary; the burden of proof falls upon the rationality of the thought and not upon its obsessive character.

Chapter IV — On the Aesthetic Character

L1 10 An acting and not a contemplative character descriptive

The individual organism is of an acting and not a contemplative character. This character is not chosen: it is constated.

L1 11 The aesthetic character as fixed point descriptive

The aesthetic character is a fixed point. With respect to fixed points there is no deliberation as to their content, but only as to the manner of acting in the face of them.

Chapter V — On Memory

L1 12 Constitutive deficiencies of memory descriptive

The individual organism presents constitutive deficiencies of memory, particularly in the recollection of the quality of inner states that have already ceased. The deficiency extends to negative states as much as to positive ones.

L1 13 The bias of the single present state descriptive

From the foregoing deficiency there follows a systematic bias: the individual organism tends to evaluate its present state as if it were the only possible state, for want of mnemonic contrast with distinct prior states.

L1 14 Bias in the evaluation of the subtraction of distress (hypothetical) descriptivehypothetical

The bias described affects particularly the evaluation of any intervention whose effect consists in the subtraction of a distress. The absence of distress is not registrable without contrast with the state that preceded it.

Title II — On the History of the Individual Organism

L1 15 The paradigmatic episodes descriptive

Certain episodes in the history of the individual organism acquire a paradigmatic character: they are not mere biographical facts but prototypical manifestations of a mechanism that will recur with variations. An episode is formative when it reveals for the first time, or in an especially pure manner, a pattern that Book Two codifies as a constant.

L1 16 Existence dependent on being imagined and valued descriptive

The individual organism conceives its existence as dependent on being imagined and valued in the representation of others. This conception is the source of the affective charge it attributes to the events of its activity, and converts each of them into a measure of its own worth before the anticipated judgment of others.

Book Two — On Functioning

morphology

Title I — On the Natures of Growth

L2 01 The three natures of growth descriptive

The individual organism does not grow by the addition of matter. It grows by virtue of three natures that traverse it: desire, habit and memory. These are not juxtaposed faculties but strata of one and the same movement: desire opens, habit consolidates, memory conserves.

L2 02 Desire, the first nature descriptive

Desire is the first nature because it is the most originary. Pleasure is not its end but the sign that the movement has oriented itself correctly; aversion to displeasure is its negative form. In this stratum the individual organism has no world: it has objects of appetite and objects of flight.

L2 03 Habit, sedimented desire descriptive

Habit is sedimented desire. Repetition reduces the cost of the movement until it becomes second nature. Habit is the first form of order: before any written norm, the body legislates by custom.

L2 04 Memory keeps habit from being mechanical descriptive

Memory keeps habit from being mechanical. Through it the individual organism has a history and not merely a functioning: it compares, anticipates, corrects. Memory generalizes, and in that gesture it prepares the rule.

L2 05 The exclusive predominance of one nature predictive

The exclusive predominance of one of the three natures produces, predictably: in desire, consumption without accumulation; in habit, fossilization; in memory, regularity without life. Growth is the articulation of the three.

Title II — On the Threefold Orientation of Desire

L2 06 The threefold orientation of desire descriptive

Desire is not homogeneous. It orients itself toward its objects according to three registers: the exception, concrete order and the norm.

L2 07 The desire for the exception descriptive

The desire for the exception wants the event: the unrepeatable, the rupture, the instant in which the rule is suspended and someone decides. Whoever desires in this register does not seek satisfaction but intensity; his pleasure is the unforeseen case.

L2 08 The desire for concrete order descriptive

The desire for concrete order wants belonging: the already-woven fabric of usages, rites and institutions within which life takes place without needing to justify itself. It is the direct relative of habit.

L2 09 The desire for the norm descriptive

The desire for the norm wants the rule as rule, independently of who decides and of what custom governs. It is the most abstract of the three: it wants the form of the law before any content.

L2 10 The orientations of desire replicate the natures descriptive hypothetical

The three orientations of desire replicate the three natures of growth: the desire for the exception is desire without mediation; that for concrete order, desire passed through habit; that for the norm, desire mediated by memory, for the rule is nothing but generalized memory.

Title III — On the Reciprocal Positivity of Pleasure and Displeasure

L2 11 Pleasure and displeasure, both positive descriptive

Pleasure and displeasure are not opposed magnitudes that subtract from one another. Both are positive.

L2 12 The differential and the integral of affect descriptive amended

Each is positive by integration of the variation of its own function. Pleasure is the total negative variation of resistance, restored to positive sign: the accumulated magnitude of its descents. Displeasure is the total positive variation of the dominion not yet attained: the accumulated magnitude of its ascents, positive in itself. Resistance overcome is a descent; pleasure is the magnitude of that descent. Dominion that recedes, or desire that expands without correlate, is an ascent of the privation; displeasure is the magnitude of that ascent.

L2 13 Instant and magnitude, not sum but accumulation descriptive amended

Neither founds the other. The relation between the instant and the magnitude is not one of subtraction but of accumulation: there is no instant in which the individual organism feels substantive pleasure; there is an accumulation that is such. The same holds, symmetrically, for displeasure.

L2 14 The error of taking the differential for the function descriptive amended

The error of one who holds that pleasure is the mere cessation of pain consists in taking the differential for the function, and the plain integral for the total variation. The integral of a negative function is negative; whoever computes it so concludes that pleasure is privative. Viewed at the instant, there is only cessation; viewed as accumulated variation restored to positive sign, there is a positive magnitude. Passive pleasure is not pleasure of lower quality: it is a differential that was never integrated. Whoever systematically suppresses all resistance does not maximize enjoyment: he sterilizes it.

L2 15 Passive pleasure as an unintegrated differential descriptive amended

Relief and pleasure are not of a different kind but of a different scale. Relief is the differential: the descent of resistance in the instant of the act. Pleasure is the integral: the variation accumulated across the series. Whoever pursues the differential believing he pursues the integral obtains true relief and obtains no pleasure: the act yields its fruit in the instant, and the series accumulates no magnitude.

L2 80 Total variation in the oscillating function descriptive

Where the function oscillates, the magnitude is not the net variation between the endpoints of the interval, but the sum of its partial variations. One who ascends and descends the same slope one hundred times has accumulated nothing by net variation, and one hundred by total variation.

Title IV — On Resistance Not Exercised

L2 16 One may lack nothing and still suffer descriptive

The individual organism may lack nothing and still suffer. The contrary force is not a lack but an alien conatus that exercises itself, whether or not there is resistance opposed to it.

L2 17 To lack is to resist, seen without interval descriptive

To lack is to resist, seen without interval. Lack is not the opposite of resistance: it is its phenomenon, and the manner in which resistance appears to the individual organism from within the instant.

L2 18 The contrary force exercises itself alone predictive

If the individual organism does not exercise its resistance, the contrary force does not cease: it exercises itself alone. The front retreats, and the retreat of the front is the administration of the individual organism by that which opposes it. Even the most immediate objects —the body, the mind— then operate against it.

L2 19 There is no long-term pleasure in passivity predictive

There is no long-term pleasure in passivity, because pleasure is the integral of resistances overcome and in passivity none is overcome. Displeasure, on the other hand, integrates: passivity does not produce an absence of affect but accumulated suffering, sedimented into habit and conserved as character.

L2 20 To exercise resistance where it may sediment into habit prescriptive

The individual organism ought to exercise its resistance there where it may sediment into habit.

L2 21 Conduct governed by immediate consequences descriptive

Conduct governed by immediate consequences is not extinguished by lucidity about its risk, for it is not controlled by the rule that states the risk but by the immediate reinforcer. To constatate the mechanism does not suspend it.

L2 22 Passivity as reinforcement administered by another descriptive

Every absence of the exercise of resistance hands over the administration of contingencies to a conatus other than one's own. Passivity is not an absence of reinforcement: it is reinforcement administered by another.

Title V — On Practical Reason and Procedural Free Will

L2 23 Own acts and external acts descriptive

In the functioning of the individual organism, own acts and external acts concur. External acts constitute the conditions; upon each external act an own act is always possible.

L2 24 All the conditions of the act are external descriptive

All the conditions of the act are external. There are no internal conditions, because there is no inside: even the most immediate object is object.

L2 25 Procedural free will descriptive

The free will of the individual organism does not consist in producing the external act, but in that, given the external act, the own act is not determined by it. Its exercise is procedural: it occurs within a procedure of acts and not above it.

L2 26 The own act is not a disposition interpretive

The own act does not constitute a disposition of any of the species enunciated in the Preliminary Title. It is practical reason, and this Code does not inquire into it beyond what is provided in the following articles.

L2 27 Practical reason operates on memory descriptive

Practical reason operates on memory. It confronts the present external conditions with the recollection, true or imagined, of previous external situations in which the own act had, or would have had, a different result. From that confrontation it produces variation in the present act.

L2 28 Practical reason is memory in act descriptive

That the recollection be falsifiable is the condition for the own act not to be determined. Practical reason is memory in act.

L2 29 Essential will and rational will descriptive

The essential will and the rational will are two operations and not two substances. Pure forms of neither of them are found. The rational will is not absolute: it requires conditions, and at times it is produced naturally and at times it produces itself by an act that takes itself as object.

L2 30 Rational determination and patience descriptive amended

The determination that exercises resistance where the outcome depends upon its own act is an act of rational will. Its exercise is not active only: it includes patience, that is, the determination that the body not move unnecessarily or detrimentally from its place.

L2 81 Passivity as the default response descriptive

Passivity in the face of prolonged aversive stimulus is not learned: it is the individual organism's default response. What is learned is control, and only through the experience that its own response alters the outcome.

L2 82 Control once learned is not preserved without exercise descriptive

Control once learned is not preserved without exercise. Where exercise ceases, the default response reappears.

Title VI — On Memory, Knowledge and its Consolidation

L2 31 Knowledge is consolidated through use descriptive

Knowledge is consolidated in the individual organism through its concrete use and not through its passive reception. Reading that is not accompanied by application, by the visualization of concrete situations, or by elaboration directed by a purpose is not fixed as a disposition by the mere fact of having been understood.

L2 32 The associative capacity and its limits descriptive

The individual organism possesses an associative capacity that operates upon diverse material and produces integrating structures of great scale, at the cost of a lesser individual consolidation of each element. This capacity is constitutive of its mode of knowing and not a defect of it.

L2 33 Attention is fixed by external pressure descriptive

The attention of the individual organism is fixed with greater efficacy when there exists an external pressure or a concrete motive that directs the study. This dependence is a structural trait of its cognitive functioning.

L2 34 The associative mechanisms of retrieval descriptive

The associative mechanisms of retrieval operate by linking insufficiently consolidated content with other content that is consolidated. They are compensatory measures and not substitutes for consolidation through use.

L2 35 Degradation is overcome by reiteration descriptive

The degradation of knowledge is overcome through the reiteration of the process that originally fixed it. There exists no autonomous mechanism of maintenance distinct from consolidation through use.

L2 36 The accumulation of consolidated knowledge descriptive

The accumulation of consolidated knowledge does not impair cognitive functioning: the greater the consolidated structure, the greater the ease of integrating new knowledge. What impairs is the accumulation of half-processed material that is neither integrated into structure nor externalized.

L2 37 Mature knowledge operates as habit descriptive

Knowledge incorporated as a disposition is not extinguished because it ceases to be articulated discursively. The most mature form of knowledge is not its constant presence in consciousness but its operation as habit.

Title VII — On the States of the Will

L2 38 The states as spherical movement descriptive

The states of the will are modifications of the fundamental spherical movement of the individual organism. Each state consists in a specific pattern of waves between the center and the periphery of the sphere. The states do not constitute discrete categories but a continuous spectrum.

L2 39 The axis between love and hatred descriptive

The central axis of the spectrum is the dynamic between love and hatred. Love is effusion from the center toward the periphery; hatred, infusion from the periphery toward the center. Both constitute the point of maximum dynamics and the leap between two worlds: one luminous and the other dark.

L2 40 The progression of the spectrum descriptive

The progression of the spectrum, from the luminous world to the dark, is the following: joy, hope, courage, love; hatred, fear, hopelessness, sadness. Between courage and love, and between hatred and fear, a qualitative leap intervenes; the other transitions are fluid. The progression is bidirectional.

L2 41 Joy and sadness, consummations of the spectrum descriptive

Joy and sadness are the consummations of the spectrum. Joy is the consummation of love; sadness, that of hatred. They are vertical asymptotes: they tend to infinity on the axis of intensity, express themselves as the only thing that is in the instant in which they manifest, and are never attained with duration.

L2 42 The normal state as horizontal asymptote descriptive

The normal state is a horizontal asymptote. It is real inasmuch as it is always present as the substrate of the other states, and virtual inasmuch as it never expresses itself on its own as a phenomenal state. It is the will reduced to pure intellective spirit.

L2 43 The four types of will descriptive

To the zones of the spectrum correspond four types of will: in sadness, the will to die bare; in hopelessness and fear, a mixture of the will to die and the will to live; in hatred and in love, the will to dominion, the most intense form of that mixture; in joy, the will to create bare; in courage and hope, the will to create mixed with the will to live.

L2 44 Love as the only positive descriptive

Love is the only positive. The absence of love is suffering. Hatred is not the contrary of love but the positivity of the negative: the will turned against itself. The will to die is what remains of the will to live when love has withdrawn completely.

L2 45 The states do not present themselves pure descriptive

The states do not present themselves pure in experience. Love infiltrates the dark world as an attenuation of hopelessness; sadness infiltrates the luminous world as melancholy within joy. The most intense states are those that contain the other within themselves the most, precisely by virtue of the magnitude of their dynamics.

L2 46 The organism undergoes the states descriptive

The individual organism undergoes the states of the will. As passive affects, they move it without its being the adequate cause of their movement: the spectrum traverses it. This servitude is a permanent condition of functioning and admits of no definitive suppression.

L2 47 The second sadness that multiplies descriptive

Upon the basal suffering there operates a second sadness, consisting in the reflexive consideration of one's own suffering and in the unfavorable interpretation of its meaning within the vital context. This operation is not added to the basal suffering: it multiplies it, and may be reiterated in successive degrees.

Title VIII — On Cycles, States of Exception and Protocols

L2 48 The type of day and unpredictability descriptive

The individual organism identifies the type of day as soon as it awakens, and cannot predict it the night before. The unpredictability does not derive from identifiable external factors but from oscillations whose periodicity has not been established. The unpredictability itself is the constant datum.

L2 49 Dysregulation from absence of structure descriptive

The individual organism experiences affective and cognitive dysregulation in periods lacking external structure or tasks of immediate execution. It manifests as restlessness, diffuse uncertainty and an impulse toward action without a defined object. Its intensity is predictably greater on non-working days.

L2 50 Physical movement as regulator predictive

Sustained physical movement constitutes a primary regulatory mechanism. In states of dysregulation from absence of structure, bodily activity fulfills a function equivalent to that fulfilled by the urgent task in periods of occupation: it provides an object that reduces the diffuse restlessness.

L2 51 The conclusions of the declines lack validity interpretive

The conclusions reached during the foreseeable affective declines lack epistemological validity, in the same way that decisions taken with a fever lack it. The individual organism knows this, and that does not suffice to withdraw itself from their force.

L2 52 The pattern of functioning and hopelessness descriptive

The individual organism experiences a recurrent pattern in which the moments of greatest functioning are followed by hopelessness. The system oscillates between the two extremes in a manner apparently independent of external circumstances. The amplitude of the range and the speed of the transitions are constitutive data, not symptoms that must be eliminated.

L2 53 The collapse after high productivity descriptive

After periods of high productivity a collapse supervenes that is produced not because things are going badly but for the contrary reason. The system lacks a circuit that processes success and translates the resulting emptiness into diffuse anguish. It is the paradox of attainment without reward.

L2 54 The ruminative cascades descriptive

The individual organism is susceptible to ruminative cascades: a specific stimulus generalizes progressively until it reaches existential themes unrelated to it. The cascade operates by affective contiguity and not by logical connection.

L2 55 Systemic solutions surpass volitional ones prescriptive

Systemic solutions are superior to volitional ones. In the face of a recurrent executive deficit, the modification of the environment is more effective than the modification of conduct. Willpower is a scarce resource in the economy of the individual organism; the design of the environment is a renewable resource.

L2 56 The confluence of maximum risk predictive

The confluence of disinhibition, unstructured time and the nocturnal hour constitutes the condition of maximum risk for the activation of compulsive conducts. The three factors operate synergistically: any one of them in isolation is manageable. Prevention must operate upon the confluence and not upon each factor in isolation.

L2 57 The protocols prescriptive

The protocols are the norm that the individual organism gives to itself, on the days when it can give it, for the days when it will not be able to.

Title IX — On Paralysis and Rationalization

L2 58 Paralyzing perfectionism descriptive

Paralyzing perfectionism is the product of the convergence of two opposing conditions and not of either of them in isolation. One demands that the task be perfect; the other does not provide the attentional energy to make it perfect. The result is not a reasonable middle point but total inaction.

L2 59 The double deficit of closure descriptive

The individual organism suffers a double deficit of closure: neither the signal of accomplishment for having completed is generated, nor the signal that what has been done is sufficiently good. The sensation of a finished task is therefore never produced.

L2 60 Paralysis inverse to complexity descriptive

The magnitude of the paralysis is inversely proportional to the intellectual complexity of the task. Mechanical tasks are more difficult than those requiring complex reasoning, because the latter generate the stimulation that activates attention and the former do not.

L2 61 Procrastination disguised as planning descriptive

The individual organism falls into procrastination disguised as planning. The drawing up of lists and priorities fulfills an anxiolytic function: it reduces the anguish produced by the accumulation of pending matters without requiring the effort of addressing them. It is compulsion disguised as executive function.

L2 62 The mechanism of subsequent rationalization descriptive

The mechanism of subsequent rationalization is the following: the intrusive thought appears first, by affective means; the intellect afterward constructs a justification that presents it as rational. The justification is not the cause of the thought but the compulsion that keeps it active.

L2 63 The constant structure of obsessive cycles descriptive

The structure of obsessive cycles is constant independently of their content. The object changes; the mechanism is identical: attentional fixation, incapacity to redirect, intellectual construction that justifies the fixation, and functional deterioration in the areas foreign to the object.

L2 64 The inversion of alarm into evidence descriptive

The inversion of alarm into evidence consists in taking the intensity of the affective response as proof of the magnitude of the risk. It is not that the risk was great and for that reason the alarm is intense: the alarm is intense and for that reason the intellect constructs the narrative that the risk was great.

L2 65 Counterfactual rumination descriptive

Counterfactual rumination consists in repeatedly reconstructing a scenario that did not occur, drawing from it affects as if it were real. The individual organism recognizes that the event did not occur and its nervous system processes it as if it did.

Title X — On Attention and Concentration

L2 66 Resistance to monofocal concentration descriptive

The individual organism experiences resistance to sustained monofocal concentration. The resistance intensifies in proportion to the duration and depth of the concentration required, and manifests itself as an impulse to reestablish cognitive contact with objects distinct from the one occupying attention.

L2 67 Resistance as expression of the associative mode descriptive

The resistance described constitutes neither an attentional defect nor evasion, but an expression of the associative cognitive mode, whose proper operation is the connection between domains. Monofocal concentration demands the transitory suspension of that mode, and this suspension is registered as loss.

L2 68 Resistance is independent of valence descriptive

The resistance operates independently of the affective valence of the object. Its cause is not the quality of the object but the exclusivity of attention upon it.

L2 69 Concentration as condition of meaning prescriptive

Sustained monofocal concentration is a necessary condition of consolidation through use and of the immersion on which the emergence of meaning depends. The individual organism must sustain it against the resistance it itself arouses, for as long as the task requires.

L2 70 The loss of contact is transitory descriptive

The sensation of loss of contact with multiplicity during concentration is transitory and does not correspond to a real loss. The unattended objects persist and remain available. The individual organism must distinguish between the functional suspension of attention and the dissolution of that to which attention is not directed.

L2 71 Regulation through interpersonal communication descriptive

The individual organism tends to seek regulation through interpersonal communication when the object of work does not provide it with immediate meaning. The meaning of the object does not precede immersion in it: it emerges from it.

L2 72 The duty to emerge from rumination prescriptive

The individual organism being of a predominantly reflexive nature, it has the duty to strive to emerge from states of rumination, whether these be pleasant or bothersome. Prolonged mental stillness without a productive object constitutes a functional risk independent of its affective valence.

Title XI — On Fog and Meta-lucidity

L2 73 Within the fog everything is indubitable descriptive

Within the fog, everything has the weight of the indubitable. A distinction is drawn between cognitive fog —ignorance, confusion, bias— and affective fog —the mood that tinges the world and is lived as a property of things and not as the color of the eye—. Both share one and the same structure.

L2 74 The fog conceals itself descriptive

The decisive trait of the fog is that it conceals itself. It does not present itself as fog but as clarity, and the obfuscation includes, as part of its content, the conviction of seeing with lucidity.

L2 75 The fog is not refuted with more content descriptive

The fog is not refuted with more content, for neither data nor reasons are lacking within it: it rearranges them all in its own favor. One emerges from it by changing position and not by changing data.

L2 76 The fog has its own truth descriptive

The fog has its own truth: that of how things matter. The error lies not in feeling but in taking the feeling for an infallible judgment about the world. The fog reveals the affective texture of the real and deceives only when it sets itself up as a tribunal.

L2 77 Meta-lucidity, to know that it is vapor prescriptive

The lucidity that is possible does not consist in dwelling permanently outside the fog, which would be a life without weight, but in remembering, while within it, that one is within. Not to dispel the vapor: to know that it is vapor.

Title XII — On the Disposition for Work

L2 78 The five characters of the disposition for work prescriptive

The mental disposition that the individual organism must adopt at the outset and during work gathers the following characters:

  1. It is cognitive: its object is intention and representation, not bodily sensation nor emotion.
  2. It is prospective: it considers future actions and not past facts.
  3. It is a posteriori: it is planned in abstract terms and one acts in order to know the real effects, without pretending to anticipate them concretely before the action.
  4. It is independent of the judgment of others: the imagination of the evaluative opinions of third parties about the person of the individual organism does not constitute a legitimate motivation of the action.
  5. It is patient: when the useful mental content does not present itself immediately, the individual organism keeps calm, allows the ideas to form, and records them when they emerge.
L2 79 The external records of formulations prescriptive

The individual organism must maintain external records of its intellectual formulations. These records supply no deficiency of the spirit: they provide the associative keys that its retrieval routes require in order to activate.

L2 83 The practice presupposes no existential assent descriptive hypothetical

The individual organism assents to no existential proposition that devotional practice might appear to presuppose.

L2 84 Efficacy depends on performance, not belief descriptive

Devotional practice is performed notwithstanding the preceding article, and its performance involves no contradiction. The condition of the practice's efficacy is its performance and not the belief of the one who performs it.

L2 85 The ground of the practice lies outside it descriptive

The ground of the practice lies outside it. It is grounded in the regulative effect it produces upon the individual organism and in the requirement laid down in Book Seven. It is not grounded in itself, nor in belief, nor in the state toward whose coming about it disposes.

Chapter II — On the Act and the State

L2 86 The mandate bears upon the act of invocation prescriptive

The individual organism shall perform devotional practice. The mandate bears upon the act of invocation.

L2 88 The state of standing before is not a habit descriptive

The state of standing before is not a habit. Habit is a property of acts and is acquired by repetition. The state is a modality of the individual organism's relation to itself, and repetition does not secure it.

L2 89 The failure of the state to come about is not a defect interpretive

The failure of the state to come about is not evidence of defect in the performance of the act, of deficiency in the individual organism, or of vice in the practice.

L2 90 Suspension of the practice and its pathological limit interpretive

Where the practice's external ground ceases, the practice is suspended. Suspension produces no distress in the individual organism; and should it produce distress, the practice has degenerated into a pathological rule and is held to be repealed.

Substance and revenue admit of a sign. The positive sign constitutes the asset; the negative, the liability. They are not two coordinate terms, but one term with an orientation.

Interest is the differential that the integral generates of itself. Where interest is proportional to capital, revenue is not constant: it grows with that which produces it.

Chapter IV. On Human Capital as an Anomalous Term

L3 08 Human capital, the anomalous term descriptive

Human capital is capital that is not alienated, produces no interest, and depreciates when unused. With respect to it, the individual organism is not owner but usufructuary of itself.

L3 09 Revenues requiring presence and civil fruits descriptive

Revenues are divided according to whether or not they require the presence of the individual organism. Remuneration is the fruit of human capital and requires presence at each instant in which it is received. Civil fruits do not require it.

L3 10 The threshold of independence descriptive

Goods relate to the organism according to three functions: the economic, by the utility they yield; the representational, by the opinion of others in which they rest; the ritual or regulatory, by the form they impose upon states of the organism. These functions may concur in one and the same good. Every good shall be classified according to its dominant function before its regime is applied. The matrix of what is had and what is produced governs the economic function only.

L3 28 Evaluation by scale interpretiveamended

The judgments of one scale and the other may diverge without contradiction. The divergence is recorded; it is not resolved by suppressing one of the scales. The imputation of expenditure is governed by the scale of the act and its regime. The relationship of holding is judged by the scale of the total vital state.

L3 29 Inverted holding interpretiveamended

Inversion is not detected by the absence of pleasure, which may be real, but by the detriment to the total vital state that the series of acts produces and by the anguish that privation of the good generates, according to the suspension test of Title XII. Expenditure under inverted holding retains its imputation according to the regime that corresponds to it. The inversion of holding is recorded as an attribute of the relationship, and its remedy is one of design, not of mandate.

§ Of liquidity

L3 15 The classification of the deficit prescriptive

A deficit is one of capacity when regular outflows exceed regular inflows. It is one of timing when the inflows of the period suffice to cover the outflows, but the date of their receipt is later than the date on which the outflows fall due. A deficit of timing tends to resolve upon the mere alignment of the dates; a deficit of capacity persists though the dates align. Every deficit shall be classified before a remedy is chosen. A deficit of timing admits of a bridge. A deficit of capacity requires the redesign of regular inflows or of regular outflows, and shall not be financed by bridge.

L3 16 Available credit is not liquidity prescriptive

Available credit is not liquidity. Available credit shall not be counted in the measurement of cash. Its use requires a deliberate decision, of which a record shall be kept.

L3 17 Flow and accumulated prescriptive

The net of the period is a flow. The accumulated balance is the sum of the flows elapsed. While the flow is negative, the accumulated balance grows, though the flow be constant. Operating decisions shall be governed by the flow of the period. The accumulated balance shall serve only to dimension the bridge.

L3 18 The reserve and the operating instrument prescriptive

When one and the same instrument finances current operations and constitutes the sole reserve, its exhaustion extinguishes the reserve without separate signal. The reserve and the operating instrument shall not be the same instrument. While they are, the instrument shall be watched daily.

L3 19 The bridge and its sources prescriptive

A bridge is the transitory financing of a deficit of timing. Its sources shall be employed in the following order: first, work of one's own of short cycle, collected upon delivery; second, the advance of one's own cash; third, income accrued but not yet received; fourth, a loan from an ally documented in a simple instrument. A deficit of timing shall not be financed with new bank debt.

L3 20 The control signal of the bridge prescriptive

The renegotiation of an obligation tends to be obtained at lesser cost before the obligation falls due; thereafter, the cost grows discontinuously. Every bridge shall bear a control date prior to the critical due date that it covers. If on that date the source has not materialized, renegotiation shall be initiated before the due date, not after it.

L3 21 Income conditioned upon a procedure prescriptive

Income whose receipt is conditioned upon a procedure of one's own is not certain while the procedure is pending. Such income shall be counted as accrued subject to procedure. The procedure upon which it depends shall be treated as an obligation with a due date of its own, prior to the projected date of receipt.

L3 22 The imputation of the deficit to a destination interpretive

A deficit whose identifiable destination is the extinction of liabilities, protected expenditure, or expenditure imputable to the deprivation regime imposed by the office or to an established trait, does not constitute dissipation. Every deficit shall be imputable to an identifiable destination. A deficit without identifiable destination is presumed to be dissipation. The presumption admits proof to the contrary. Expenditure is classified as protected, structural, and flexible. Protected expenditure shall not be reduced in order to service liabilities. Reduction shall always begin with flexible expenditure. The determination of which expenditures are protected belongs to Book Two.

L3 24 Structural complementary income interpretive

When base income does not cover regular outflows, complementary income is structural. Structural complementary income shall not be treated as extraordinary, nor shall its receipt be counted as an improvement of the regime.

Book Four — On Personal Relations

self and other

Title I — On the War of All Against All

L4 01 The war of all against all descriptive

In the absence of a mediating institution, the bond between two individual organisms is governed by reciprocal distrust, competition and the search for precedence.

L4 02 The contracting parties owe one another before the pact interpretive

The contracting parties owe one another loyalty, reciprocity and trust before having agreed to anything. This owing does not come from outside the bond: it arises from the situation itself and requires no third party to declare it.

L4 03 Each party is judge of its own play descriptive

There being no third party, each party is judge of its own play. The adjudication is internal and subsequent to the act, and takes the form of satisfaction or of aversion.

L4 04 The judge is a party descriptive

The absence of a third party does not entail the absence of judgment. It entails that the judge is a party.

L4 20 The instance is not a third party interpretive

«The instance of which § III bis of the Preliminary Title treats is not a third party. It is measure and not tribunal: it supplies the standard according to which the play is judged, and does not judge it. Each party remains judge of its own play.»

L4 21 A third party occupies a position; the instance does not descriptive

«A third party is a term of the relation: it acts and is acted upon, occupies a position in the field, and is a possible object of the will to dominion. The instance occupies no position.»

L4 22 What occupies no position is neither ally nor adversary descriptive

«What occupies no position can be neither ally nor adversary. The instance does not intervene in the personal relation, does not arbitrate it, and does not modify it.»

Title II — On Relations of Power

L4 05 In every relation there is a relation of power descriptive

In every personal relation a relation of power is established. One exercises it and the other undergoes it; one enjoys the power exercised and the other suffers its exercise.

L4 06 The asymmetry of power is constitutive descriptive

The asymmetry of power is constitutive of the bond and not an accident of it. It does not invert automatically nor is it resolved by the mere awareness of the parties.

L4 07 Power belongs to whoever least needs recognition descriptive

The position of power belongs to whoever least needs the recognition of the other. The need for recognition is, therefore, the measure of the servile position, and not force.

L4 08 The will to dominion descriptive

The will to dominion is the most intense form of the mixture between the will to live and the will to die. It occupies, in the spectrum of the states, the zone of the axis between love and hatred.

L4 09 Not to betray the loyalty of the powerful prescriptive

The individual organism must not betray the loyalty of the powerful persons who have given it their trust. This duty intensifies in proportion to the power of the person who trusts, for proximity to power makes betrayal feasible as a strategy and, therefore, its execution graver.

Title III — On Reciprocity and its Forms

L4 10 Verifiable reciprocity as reinforcer descriptive

The effective reinforcer of the bond is verifiable reciprocity. The imagined substitutes are weak and unstable reinforcers, and their collapse is foreseeable.

L4 11 Reciprocity is not symmetry descriptive

Reciprocity is not symmetry. Asymmetry of initiative, of need and of power subsists even in the bonds that endure. Reciprocity requires that the asymmetry be borne, not that it be suppressed.

L4 12 Obsessive attachment to persons perceived as exceptional descriptive

The individual organism presents a recurrent pattern of obsessive attachment to persons perceived as exceptional. The arc is constant: idealization, disproportionate affective investment, frustration at the lack of reciprocation, and a feeling of unworthiness. The object changes; the mechanism is invariable.

L4 13 Idealization operates through projection descriptive

Idealization operates through projection: the individual organism relates to an image of its own deposited upon the person rather than to the real person. The recognition of the projection produces a motivational collapse, for the energy that the bond provided collapses with it.

L4 14 To diversify the objects of interest prescriptive

The most effective way to obtain something from another person is not to become obsessed with it. To avoid obsession with a single object, the individual organism must deliberately diversify its objects of interest, so that mental energy does not fall upon one alone.

L4 15 Certain presences regulate by themselves descriptive

Certain presences regulate the individual organism by themselves, independently of the content of the relation. This function is irreducible to friendship, to counsel and to affection: it is an effect of presence upon the nervous system.

Title IV — On Friendship

L4 16 Friendship, the highest species of relation descriptive

Friendship is the highest species of personal relation. It admits of three forms, which may concur and are not subordinated to one another: that of companions, founded on shared activity; that of peers, founded on an equality real or agreed; and romantic friendship.

L4 17 The friendship of companions descriptive

The friendship of companions lasts as long as the activity that founds it. Its ending is not betrayal but the exhaustion of its cause.

L4 18 The friendship of peers descriptive

The friendship of peers is rare, because the equality that founds it is not declared: it is verified.

L4 19 Romantic friendship descriptive

Romantic friendship is the only one that seeks to close the constitutive asymmetry of the bond through the identification of two wills. It is the highest form and the one of greatest accumulated cost, and of it this Code says that it exists and inquires no further.

Book Five — On Social Relations

Title I — On the War of All Against All in Social Form

L5 01 The war of all against all in social form descriptive

Among collectivities that recognize no common institution, reciprocal distrust, competition and the search for precedence prevail, just as among individual organisms.

L5 02 The incipient mediations descriptive

The incipient mediations of this state are custom, force and reputation. None of them constitutes an institution, and none suffices to determine which play was correct.

L5 03 The necessity of the pact and the enclosure descriptive

From the insufficiency of the incipient mediations emerges the necessity of the pact. The enclosure that the pact establishes protects the weak from the strong, the dissenter from power, and the individual organism from fusion into the whole.

L5 04 The enclosure is the condition of every bridge descriptive

The enclosure is the condition of every bridge. Only one who is protected from intrusion can open to the other out of enjoyment and not out of necessity.

Title II — On the Family

L5 05 The family, first instituted structure descriptive

The family is the first instituted social structure, and it is prior to every theory that would explain it. It is not pacted: it is dwelt in.

L5 06 The functions of the family descriptive

The functions proper to the family are reproduction, transmission, care and primary socialization.

L5 07 The competence-limit of the family descriptive

The family cannot govern anything situated outside itself without passing to the next layer. Its limit is not one of power but of competence.

Title III — On Civil Society

L5 08 Civil society descriptive

Civil society is the layer where labor, commercial and professional relations occur. Its typical bond is the contract, and its fundamental mediation is labor.

L5 09 The labor relation is contractual descriptive

The labor relation is contractual: the parties negotiate, within limits, the terms of their bond.

L5 10 Civil society does not resolve its own conflicts descriptive

Civil society cannot resolve by itself the conflicts it engenders, for it lacks a third party to declare which play was correct. That lack, and not the wickedness of its members, is what opens the institutional layer.

L6 01 The lamina, scale of the will's deployment interpretive

By lamina is understood each of the scales in which the will deploys itself with identical structure: that of the Ego, that of the Organization and that of the State.

L6 02 The four positions of the lamina interpretive

Title I — On Contemplation and the Tension that Inhabits It

L7 01 Contemplation as suspension of the will descriptive

Aesthetic contemplation operates as a transitory suspension of the will. For its duration, the states of the will cease to move the individual organism and become the object of its consideration. What in Book Two is undergone as passive affect is, in contemplation, understood as active affect.

L7 02 Suspension, not elimination of the will descriptive

Suspension is not the elimination of the will but the temporary stilling of its operation. Once contemplation concludes, the will resumes its course. The cessation of suffering that it procures is intermittent and does not admit of being converted into a permanent state.

L7 03 The axiom of resistance against contemplation descriptive

The axiom of resistance is opposed to the suspension of the will. The will, whose elemental operation is to conserve the present state, registers its own stilling as a threat. From this it follows that contemplation is resisted at its entrance, because it entails the cessation of the constant operation, and at its exit, because it entails the loss of the state attained.

L7 04 The symmetry of the two resistances descriptive

Both resistances are operations of the same conatus, and their symmetry reveals their common nature. The conatus does not defend a determinate content but the persistence of the present state, whatever it may be.

L7 05 The opposition as dynamic core interpretive

The opposition between the axiom of resistance and the doctrine of contemplation constitutes neither a defect of the system nor a contradiction to be resolved: it is its dynamic core. The doctrine of happiness is formulated not against the will but with it.

L7 06 To enter, sustain and release contemplation prescriptive

It falls to the individual organism to enter into contemplation despite the resistance it arouses in it, to sustain it throughout its course, and to permit its ending without seeking to retain it beyond its own duration. The pretension to permanence converts contemplation into a new form of servitude.

L7 07 The affect is traversed, not suppressed descriptive

The contemplated affect is not suppressed: it is traversed. The aesthetic way repositions the individual organism with respect to the affect, such that the affect ceases to be the pilot and becomes the landscape.

Title II — On the Foundations of the Beautiful

L7 08 The two orders of the beautiful descriptive

The beautiful is founded upon two distinguishable orders: one subjective, consisting in the perception of regularity, symmetry and proportion of the parts with respect to the whole; and one objective, consisting in the fulfillment of an end with grace and adequacy. Both may concur in one and the same object, or one may predominate over the other according to the nature of the activity contemplated.

L7 09 The subjectively beautiful is not arbitrary descriptive

The subjectively beautiful is not an arbitrary projection. The regularity and symmetry perceived are analogous to real patterns of harmonic movement. In the arts where the subjectively beautiful predominates, symmetry and regularity constitute the elemental units of the more complex patterns that appear in the other arts.

L7 10 The richness of aesthetic experience descriptive

Concrete aesthetic experience is rarely founded upon a single order. One and the same experience may simultaneously activate the register of formal regularity, that of vital tension, that of sensible matter, that of figurative form, and that of musical structure. The richness of an aesthetic experience is a function of the number and the intensity of the registers it activates.

L7 11 Evaluation according to the scale of organization descriptive

The aesthetic evaluation of an element depends on the scale of organization at which it is considered. An element dissonant at one scale may be a functional component of a beautiful pattern at a higher scale. The ugly possesses no autonomous aesthetic value; its incorporation is justified only when it is integrated into a regularity of greater order that contains it and confers function upon it.

L7 12 The ugly represented and the ugliness of the work descriptive

The ugly represented in a work is not the same as the ugliness of the work. The ugliness of the object represented is material of the composition; the foundation of beauty resides in the form with which that material is organized.

L7 13 Contemplation extends to all form descriptive

Aesthetic contemplation extends to every human activity endowed with form. Processes, procedures and institutions are susceptible of aesthetic appreciation insofar as they present formal regularity, adequacy to their ends, or both.

Title III — On Music and Phenomenological Analysis

L7 14 Music, privileged place among the arts descriptive

Music occupies a privileged place among the arts. It does not represent the will through conceptual mediations: it expresses it directly. The movements of a composition—effusions, infusions, leaps, accelerations, waves—are the very movements of the will described in Book Two.

L7 15 Music makes the spectrum audible descriptive

Music makes the complete spectrum audible and allows the individual organism to traverse it without undergoing it. This formulation is not metaphorical but phenomenologically verifiable.

L7 16 Atmospheric music and music of impact descriptive

Atmospheric music generates contemplative distance; music of impact generates passive affect, for it submerges without sustaining. The first is contemplation; the second, immersion.

L7 17 The phenomenological analysis of the work interpretive

The phenomenological analysis of a work consists in identifying which states of the will manifest themselves in each section and how they transit among one another. It is irreducible to technical analysis: one and the same harmonic progression may express distinct states according to context, and one and the same state may manifest itself through diverse techniques.

L7 18 The normal state as sections of rest descriptive

In music, the normal state manifests itself as the sections of rest: the silence that allows the intellect to recover in order to comprehend the ensuing tension. Its function is analogous to that which it fulfills in the spectrum.

L7 19 Works that travel and works that dwell descriptive

Works divide into two genera according to their relation to the spectrum: those that travel, which cross from one territory to another, and those that dwell, which remain in the zone of the axis without leaving it, displaying the two modes of being at the dynamic center.

L7 20 The asymmetric resolution of the journey descriptiveamended

The resolution of the journey is asymmetric. The work that travels from the luminous world does not resolve: it remains at the axis. The work that travels from the dark world does resolve: it arrives at sadness, which is consummation. This confirms that the will to die is what remains of the will to live.

Title IV — On Creation

L7 21 Syncretism as creative mode descriptive

The creative mode proper to the individual organism is syncretism: the combination of conceptual, intuitive and technical elements from diverse disciplines, where no isolated element attains excellence but their combination produces a value that exceeds the sum of the parts.

L7 22 The intuitive layer, irreplaceable contribution descriptive

In every syncretic work, the irreplaceable contribution of the individual organism is the intuitive layer: the decision of which elements belong together and why. The concept may come from a philosopher, the technique from a tool, the sensible matter from a prior artist. The vision that connects them is an act of creation of its own, irreducible to its sources.

L7 23 To desire creation while new things remain prescriptiveamended

The individual organism ought to desire and seek creation for as long as the possibility of new things exists. This possibility tends toward infinity. Only at the moment when nothing new remains to be created is it fitting to desire death, which is then not renunciation but consummation.

L7 24 To resist the anticipation of others' indifference prescriptive

The individual organism ought to resist the anticipation of others' indifference as a motive for not creating. The need for recognition is a state of the will—a form of love, an effusion that seeks to pass beyond the individual sphere—and as such it is legitimate. Nevertheless, creation ought not to be subordinated to the obtaining of recognition, for such subordination transforms the will to create into the will to dominion and distorts the work.

L7 25 Acting and contemplating organisms descriptive

There are acting organisms and contemplating organisms. Both execute aesthetic activity, and neither attains the ultimate end with greater truth than the other. The plurality of modes follows from the real plurality of wills: each perspective is its own, and the perspectives contend and yet may be integrated.

Title V — On the Ways of Happiness and their Hierarchy

L7 26 The lag between desire and attainment descriptive

The lag between the temporality of desire and the temporality of attainment is constitutive. The individual organism desires with immediacy what can only be obtained through execution projected in time. This lag admits not of resolution but of toleration.

L7 27 The containment of discomfort prescriptive

The duty to bear the discomfort deriving from the lag is fulfilled not through its elimination, which is impossible, but through its containment. Containment may be corporal, occupational or intellectual; it does not suppress the discomfort but renders it compatible with functioning.

L7 28 Detachment from everyday distress prescriptive

In the face of the events of everyday activity, the individual organism shall adopt a disposition of detachment with respect to the distress that such events generate through the thoughts and feelings associated with them.

L7 29 Attention to the ultimate motives prescriptive

The detachment prescribed does not entail inattention to the ultimate motives whose satisfaction the activity pursues. The individual organism shall keep those motives in view at all times, without making them, either, an object of attachment.

L7 30 Detached action brings one nearer to the end predictive

Action detached from everyday distress, with attention to the ultimate motives preserved, brings the individual organism mediately nearer to the satisfaction of those motives. Attachment to distress subtracts efficacy from action; attention to the motives confers finality upon it.

L7 31 The motor is purpose, not well-being descriptive

The functional motor of the individual organism is not well-being but purpose. Hopelessness is not broken with calm but with meaning. When purpose is active, the individual organism functions even under conditions of distress; when it is deactivated, objective well-being does not suffice to generate movement.

L7 32 The passage from contemplation to action interpretive

The passage from contemplation to practical action does not constitute a degradation of the knowledge that has been effectively consolidated through its use. One who has internalized a truth and acts without holding it present in consciousness has not lost it: he is exercising it.

L7 33 The fear of losing wisdom interpretive

The fear of losing wisdom by devoting oneself to practical tasks is partly founded and partly unfounded. It is unfounded with respect to consolidated knowledge, which operates as habit independently of its presence in consciousness. It is founded with respect to formulations that did not attain sufficient consolidation. The rational measure is not to avoid practical action in order to safeguard contemplation, but to secure consolidation and the record before passing from the one to the other.

L7 34 All happiness is mediated by constitution interpretive

All happiness of the individual organism is mediated by its constitution. The question of whether a happiness is genuine or artificial is ill-formulated: there is no immediate happiness of which the others would be a falsification.

L7 35 The hierarchy of the ways of happiness interpretive

The ways of happiness are ordered in a hierarchy, not by their authenticity but by their sustainability and by the cost they accumulate. They are, in order: the significant human bond with verifiable reciprocity; purpose and the intellectual project; aesthetic contemplation; and intervention upon the substrate. None is illegitimate; the last is the most costly, and its cost tends to grow.

L7 36 The possibility of reopening accumulation descriptive

So long as the individual organism exists, the capacity to exercise resistance anew persists, even if it does not exercise it and even if it does not notice it. From this it follows that in any circumstance whatever, ruin included, it is possible to reopen accumulation.

L7 37 The Code fulfills a eudemonological function interpretive

This Code fulfills a eudemonological function. It transforms what is undergone into datum, the datum into article, and the article into system. The process does not eliminate suffering: it repositions it, and it ceases to be pure undergoing to become the material of a work.

Subjects — Index

cross-cutting concepts

Accumulation
The relation between instant and affective magnitude, understood as accumulation of infinitesimal differentials rather than sum, the logic by which growth admits degrees and may be reopened.
Act and fruit
The distinction between the act, which the organism governs and owes, and the fruit, which depends on circumstances beyond it and is awaited rather than owed.
Action and contemplation
A distinction between acting and contemplating organisms, two equally valid modes of aesthetic power, with the individual's character inclining toward action without denying contemplation's dignity.
Actual and latent
The binary distinction between what imposes itself (actual, like substance) and what is noticed only through comparison over an interval (latent, like revenue), an irreversible polarity.
Adjudication
The judgment of what is already done, exercised upon the concrete order; in personal relations each party judges its own play, while institutional relations require a third-party judge.
Administrative organism
The functional unit through which the State's institutional will is deployed, simultaneously pursuing the State's end and its own institutional interest.
Aesthetic character
A fixed point of individual constitution, acting rather than contemplative and oriented to creation, exempt from deliberation about its content or reform.
Aesthetic contemplation
A transitory suspension of the will in which passive affect becomes an object of active consideration; tension is traversed, not eliminated, as the dynamic core of experience.
Aesthetic experience
A stratified, plural phenomenon in which a single experience may activate multiple registers at once, its richness growing with their concurrence.
Aesthetic registers
The distinct orders an experience of beauty can activate — formal regularity, vital tension, sensible matter, figurative form, musical structure — whose concurrence deepens the experience.
Aesthetics of form
The extension of aesthetic contemplation to any activity endowed with form — processes, procedures, institutions — wherever formal regularity or fitness to ends appears.
Affective alarm
An intense emotional reaction to perceived danger, whose risk lies in inverting alarm intensity into "evidence" of the danger's magnitude, feeding rationalization and anxiety.
Affective contiguity
The principle by which mental contents link through shared emotional tone rather than logical connection, explaining ruminative cascades from minor incidents to existential themes.
Affective cycles
The recurrent, unpredictable oscillation between high functioning and hopelessness in the individual organism, whose amplitude and speed are constitutive data, not symptoms.
Affective spectrum
The continuum of will-states from a luminous world (joy, hope, courage, love) to a dark one (hatred, fear, hopelessness, sadness), centered on the love-hatred axis.
Affective texture
The way things matter as revealed by feeling rather than judgment; legitimate information about weight, deceiving only when mistaken for infallible judgment.
Asset and liability
The positive or negative sign borne by substance and revenue in the organism's accounting, a single oriented magnitude rather than two symmetrical terms.
Associative mode
The organism's proper cognitive mode of connecting across domains rather than following linear sequence, pathological only when material exceeds integrative capacity.
Asymmetry
A constitutive, not accidental, inequality of position or power within a relation, which mature reciprocity governs well rather than eliminates.
Attainment without reward
The collapse that follows success rather than failure, arising from an altered reward system lacking a circuit to process attainment, turning success into diffuse anguish.
Attention
A structural trait by which attention is fixed more effectively by external pressure or concrete motive than by will, forming with energy a scarce, non-transferable economy.
Attentional fixation
Attention captured by an object it cannot detach from, the constant first moment underlying the structure of obsessive cycles regardless of their content.
Authorship
Resides in the directing vision of a work rather than its manual execution, an irreplaceable intuitive contribution that a pseudonym veils but does not annul.
Being, having and representing
The cardinal Schopenhauerian distinction of Book Three among what the organism is, has, and represents, governed respectively by different Books of the Code.
Body and mind
The two particular organs of the individual organism, distinct particular wills that offer their own resistance rather than constituting the organism itself.
Bond by enjoyment
Openness to another born of enjoyment rather than need, possible only after an enclosure that protects from intrusion, since the wall precedes the bridge.
Bridge (financing)
The transitory financing that covers a timing deficit between outflows falling due and inflows received, drawing on an ordered set of sources.
Causality
The category by which the Code reads connections between states, distinguishing efficient and final causes and insisting that temporal precedence alone never founds a causal bond.
Change of position
Exiting mental "fog" not by accumulating more data or reasons but by shifting one's vantage point on one's own situation.
Civil society
The layer of labor, commerce and the professions bonded typically by contract, unable to resolve its own conflicts and thus requiring a third party.
Closure signal
The internal signal of a task's completion and sufficiency, doubly absent in the organism, leaving the reward system without adjudication and feeding paralysis.
Compulsion
Conduct governed by immediate reinforcers rather than rules, unaffected by lucidity about its risk and later clothed in rational justification.
Conditions of risk
External circumstances and organism states that, in confluence, make a compulsive conduct foreseeable; the Code treats them as objects of prevention.
Conditions of the act
What the Code describes instead of prescribing acts: the external conditions under which an act becomes possible, since there is no interior core.
Consolidation through use
Knowledge is fixed in the organism through concrete application and purposeful elaboration, not passive reception, eventually operating as habit.
Constitution
The singular neurological and affective form through which an individual organism processes all experience; the task is to know and work with it, not correct it.
Containment
The treatment of an ineliminable discomfort by holding it within limits — corporal, occupational or intellectual — that keep functioning possible, rather than suppressing it.
Contract
The bond whose terms parties negotiate, proper to civil society (e.g. the labor relation), contrasted with the statutory relationship where terms are fixed by the institution.
Control
A transversal function exercised by the judiciary and analogous bodies (constitutional review, audit, ombudsman) that adjudicates what is already done upon the concrete order.
Counterfactual
A representation of events that did not occur but which the nervous system processes affectively as if real, producing sterile counterfactual rumination.
Creation
The aesthetic way of happiness desired while new things remain possible, pursued through syncretism — combining elements from diverse disciplines into a value exceeding their sum.
Debt
Measured by its exponent (the law of its growth) rather than its amount at any instant, functioning as the negative sign of interest that consumes rather than generates.
Decision
The irreducible instant, in Schmitt's decisionist sense, in which the norm is suspended and someone resolves before the unforeseen, a residue of will the norm cannot absorb.
Decision, norm and concrete order
The three coordinate, serial moments of juridical thought — decision at the origin, norm mediating, concrete order at the periphery — reproduced within the administrative organism.
Deficit of timing and of capacity
The two kinds of financial deficit — capacity, where outflows structurally exceed inflows, and timing, where sufficient inflows arrive too late — each needing a distinct remedy.
Deprivation regime of the office
The imputation to the office of substitution expenditures incurred when the office suppresses a class of goods, an identifiable destination that does not count as dissipation.
Design of the environment
The principle that modifying the environment, a renewable resource, is more effective than modifying the will, a scarce one, so systemic solutions surpass volitional ones.
Desire
The first and most original nature of growth, oriented toward objects of appetite and flight via three registers (exception, concrete order, norm), sedimenting into habit.
Detachment
A qualified way of happiness releasing the organism from reactive everyday distress while keeping sustained attention on ultimate motives, so detached action draws nearer to the end.
Devotional practice
A regulative technique performed for its effect on the organism regardless of belief, hypothetical and non-obligatory, suspended without distress when its external ground ceases.
Differential and integral
The calculus-derived matrix by which pleasure and displeasure are understood as integrals of moment-by-moment differentials of resistance overcome or dominion unattained.
Dignity
The principle that every particular action is an end and not solely a means, so each technical unit or administrative organism has its own dignity alongside serving the whole.
Directing idea
The founding idea of a work to be realized that precedes organized power and communion, giving an institution unity and permanence; its loss corrupts the institution.
Disposition for work
The mental disposition required at the outset and during work, bearing five characters: cognitive, prospective, a posteriori, independent of others' judgment, and patient.
Dissipation
A deficit lacking an identifiable destination, presumed dissipation unless proven otherwise, as opposed to deficits imputed to liabilities, protected expenditure, or the deprivation regime.
Diversification of desire
The technique of deliberately distributing interest across multiple objects to avoid obsessive attachment to a single one, restoring optimal distance and power.
Doctrine and sources
The principle that the doctrinal writings grounding the Code's provisions are not part of it and do not bind its interpreter, who is bound only to the articles.
Double inversion
A two-step reassessment whereby an apparent dissipation is first rehabilitated as a real, justified fruit, then revealed as serving the strategic conservation of the good.
Dynamic core
The tension between the axiom of resistance and the doctrine of contemplation, preserved rather than dissolved as the generative principle of movement within the system.
Dysregulation
The affective and cognitive disorder — restlessness, diffuse uncertainty, aimless impulse — that arises in periods lacking external structure, the reverse of external structure.
Economic analysis of law
The quantitative complement (magnitudes, frequencies, costs) that accompanies the Code's essentially qualitative, juridical method without substituting for or displacing it.
Emergence of meaning
Meaning emerges from sustained, monofocal immersion in an object rather than being imposed from without, and also arises through regulated interpersonal communication.
End and means
The relation between what is pursued and what is employed for it; corruption occurs when means usurp the place of ends, as in treating office as mere interest.
Epistemological validity
The property, lacking in conclusions reached during foreseeable moods of decline, that allows a conclusion to be held as reliable knowledge.
Equality
The parity founding the friendship of peers, which is not declared but verified through dealing, underlying reciprocity between equals of the same rank.
Equilibrium and predominance
In ternary structures, one term's predominance over the others predictably produces consumption, fossilization, or emptying; health lies in equilibrium, pathology in predominance.
Essential will and rational will
Two operations of the organism, not two substances, following Tönnies's Wesenwille/Kürwille distinction, recurring across social layers and the institutional will.

The ethics immanent to a personal bond, whereby contracting parties owe one another loyalty, reciprocity, and trust before any agreement, adjudicated by the parties themselves.
Ethology of the organism
The descriptive study of what the organism is and does — its constant behaviors and mechanisms — paired with morphology, which describes what it is made of.
Evaluation by scale
Every act and relationship with a good is judged at two irreducible scales, tactical (the singular act) and strategic (the total vital state), whose divergent judgments are recorded, not resolved.
Everyday distress
The small suffering generated by daily events and the thoughts attached to them, met with a disposition of detachment rather than denial or struggle.
Exception, order and norm
The three registers orienting desire: toward the unrepeatable event, toward the woven fabric of concrete order and belonging, and toward the rule as rule.
External pressure
Attention is fixed more effectively when an external pressure or concrete motive directs study, so scaffolding and purpose achieve regulation the will alone cannot.
External records
The organism's intellectual formulations deposited outside itself, the externalized third nature, maintained to supply associative keys for later retrieval.
External structure
Imposed schedules and frameworks that regulate the organism from without; their absence produces affective and cognitive dysregulation, especially on non-working days.
Family
The first instituted social structure, prior to any theory explaining it — not pacted but dwelt in — with its own functions and a competence-limit.
Finality
The final cause admitted by the Code alongside the efficient cause, ordering conduct toward ends arranged in a series where nearer ends draw content from more remote ones.
Fixed points
Constitutive data, such as the aesthetic character, that admit no deliberation about their content — only about how one acts before them, since they are received, not chosen.
Flow and accumulated
The period's net flow versus the cumulative sum of past flows; operating decisions follow the flow while the accumulated balance only sizes the bridge.
Fog
A state of obfuscation that conceals itself, presenting as lucidity while everything within it feels indubitable; the Code exists largely to be read when it closes in.
Form and composition
Beauty resides in the form organizing represented material, not in the material itself; a work may depict the horrible and still be beautiful through its composition.
Free will
The organism's free will lies not in producing the external act but in the own act not being determined by it, exercised procedurally within a procedure of acts.
Friendship
The highest species of personal relation, admitting three concurrent forms: companionship founded on shared activity, peer equality, and romantic friendship.
Function of the Code
The Code exists so the organism, when unable to notice its own position, may read the one it occupied when it could — a eudemonological, meta-lucid instrument.
Functional risk
The harm to functioning produced by prolonged mental stillness without a productive object, regardless of whether the state feels pleasant or unpleasant.
Functions of the State
The three functions arising from the essential will — memory legislates, habit executes, liking adjudicates — constituting a separation of functions rather than of powers.
Goods of ritual function
Goods whose value lies in the form they impose on formless tension, converting it into a bounded sequence, valued by determination and cost rather than enjoyment.
Growth
Growth is the integration of resistance: desire opens it, habit consolidates it, and memory preserves it as rule, with exclusive predominance of any one nature being pathological.
Habit
Sedimented desire, the second nature of growth; repetition reduces the cost of movement until it becomes automatic, consolidating resistance short of memory's fixity.
Happiness
A state mediated by the organism's constitution, not an immediate datum; its ways are ordered by sustainability and cost rather than by authenticity.
Harmonic patterns
The real regularities of harmonic movement to which perceived beauty, symmetry, and regularity correspond, grounding the subjectively beautiful rather than making it arbitrary.
Hierarchy of expenditure
The classification of expenditure into protected, structural, and flexible orders, where reduction always begins with flexible expenditure to preserve what is protected.
Hierarchy of ways
The ordering of the ways of happiness by sustainability and cost — human bond, purpose, aesthetic contemplation, and intervention upon the substrate — not by moral worth.
History of the organism
The organism's trajectory is not an indifferent succession of facts but a periodizable history with paradigmatic episodes that reveal recurring mechanisms.
Holdings and income

Human capital
The anomalous good that is not alienated, produces no interest, and depreciates when unused, of which the organism is usufructuary rather than owner.
Hypothetical dispositions
Dispositions whose truth-value remains provisional, neither excluded from the Code nor diminished in usefulness, carrying a duty to seek verification when possible.
Idealization
Relating to a projected image deposited upon a person rather than to the real person, whose recognition produces motivational collapse as the first moment of obsessive attachment.
Immediate reinforcer
The proximate consequence that governs conduct independently of awareness of its risk, since behavior is controlled by the immediate reinforcer rather than by the rule.
Imputation of the deficit
Every deficit must be traced to an identifiable destination — extinguished liabilities, protected expenditure, or established traits — else it is presumed dissipation.
Incipient mediations
The pre-institutional forms — custom, force, and reputation — that attenuate the war of all against all in social form without yet attaining institutional stability.
Income
Stub note superseded by the renamed terminology "revenue".
Independence
The threshold at which fruits not requiring presence suffice for subsistence, so that the organism's resistance is no longer conscripted by revenue.
Institution
A moral person with a directing idea, an organized power in its service, and a communion of participants, prior to the norm that governs it.
Integration
The organism's joining with the external to form a higher-order organism without suppressing the plurality of the integrated wills — composing without annulling.
Intellectual work
The conversion of inalienable human capital into alienable capital that bears fruit without the organism's presence, making the work both a good and part of the author.
Intelligence
An attribute functioning simultaneously as resource and risk factor, enabling both strategic understanding and elaborate justification of compulsive conduct.
Interest
The revenue that substance produces by its mere existence, proportional to capital and growing with it, the positive counterpart of debt.
Interpretation
The canon by which the Code's dispositions are read in light of one another according to their species, so that descriptive and prescriptive norms coexist without contradiction.
Intoned invocation
The act uniting word and musical form, where word presupposes the adjudicating tribunal and musical form renders sensible the vantage from which a series is seen as a series.
Inverted holding
When the organism, believing it possesses a good, instead serves the conservation of its relationship to it, detected by harm to the total vital state, not absent pleasure.
Is and ought
The methodical separation between dispositions stating what is and those prescribing what ought to be, forbidding derivation of one from the other without mediation.
Joy and sadness
The consummations of love and hatred respectively, vertical asymptotes on the axis of intensity that are never reached with duration.
Knowledge
The organism's cognitive material, consolidated through concrete use rather than passive reception, limited by associative capacity, and restored through reiteration rather than accumulation.
Labor relation
The contractual, typical bond of civil society mediated by labor and remuneration, opposed to the statutory relationship where duties are fixed without negotiation.
Laminae
The scales at which the will deploys itself with identical structure — Ego, Organization, and State — each generated recursively and bearing four positions.
Learned helplessness
The default subcortical response to prolonged aversive stimulation, distinct from the passivity that is learned; only cortical experience of control restores the response.
Legislation
The State's act of giving norms to itself — rational will visualized as norm — with the legislator conceived from the practice of administration rather than in the abstract.
Limit of the organism
The organism's extent is determined not anatomically but by the site where its resistance is exercised; property extends this limit and integration displaces it.
Liquidity
The capacity to meet regular obligations on their due date, distinct from available credit, with operating decisions governed by the period's flow rather than the accumulated balance.
Love and hatred
The two poles of the affective spectrum's central axis: love as effusion from center to periphery and the only positive, hatred as infusion toward the center.
Loyalty
The bond owed to the powerful, a rule of prudence not to be betrayed, though within institutions it has a limit and does not bind against the institution's directing idea.
Meaning
The organism's true functional motor beyond well-being, emerging from immersion in an object rather than preceding it, discovered by working rather than deliberating.
Memory
A constitutionally deficient faculty, especially regarding recalled quality of ceased inner states, yet also the ground of practical reason and the safeguard keeping habit from mere mechanism.
Meta-lucidity
The lucidity possible within confusion: not escaping the fog permanently but remembering, while inside it, that one is inside it.
Method of the Code
An eminently qualitative technique specifying objects by character, degree and modality, complemented but not replaced by quantitative specification where needed.
Misalignment and management
Reframes the neurological condition as a manageable discordance between functional complexity and its conditions, not an incapacity, calling for deliberate administration.
Monofocal concentration
Sustained single-object attention that the organism resists in proportion to required depth, a necessary condition for consolidation and the emergence of meaning.
Moral right
Recognition, not concession, of intellectual work's dual nature as both good and part of the author, persisting even after the embodied capital is alienated.
Morphology of the organism
The structural form the organism's mechanisms draw in operation, the object of predictive dispositions that anticipate constant causes and probable effects.
Music
A privileged aesthetic art that expresses the will's movements directly rather than representing them conceptually, making the entire affective spectrum audible.
Musical form
A lent scale: rhythm, tone, tempo and cadence unfold temporal tension and deferred resolution rather than representing objects, making sensible that immediate relief is not pleasure.
Neurological condition
A constitutive datum understood as manageable misalignment rather than incapacity, where diagnosis functions as naming rather than verdict.
Norm and conduct
Distinguishes prescriptive normatives' validity from their contingent efficacy; the gap between what a rule commands and what practice performs does not invalidate the rule.
Normal state
The horizontal asymptote of the affective spectrum, real as ever-present substrate yet virtual since never phenomenally expressed alone; in music it appears as restful sections.
Obsessive attachment
A recurrent arc toward persons perceived as exceptional — idealization, disproportionate investment, frustration, felt unworthiness — whose antidote is diversifying objects of interest.
Obsessive cycles
A constant structure — attentional fixation, inability to redirect, justifying construction, and functional deterioration elsewhere — that recurs regardless of the object involved.
Operating reserve
The reserve held against the instrument of current operation, which must not be the same instrument lest its exhaustion silently extinguish the reserve.
Order and custom
The pre-existing web of usages, rites and institutions — rooted in habit as sedimented desire — within which life unfolds unjustified, with the family as its first inhabited form.
Originality
Resides in the unitary vision governing selected materials rather than in the novelty of the materials themselves, making even syncretism fully original.
Paradigmatic episodes
Biographical facts that become prototypical, revealing for the first time or most purely a recurring mechanism the Code later codifies as constant.
Paralysis
Total inaction before a task caused by the convergence of perfectionist demand and insufficient attentional energy, aggravated by a double deficit of closure signals.
Passive and active affects
Following Spinoza, passive affects are undergone without adequate causation while active affects are traversed rather than suppressed.
Passivity
The absence of exercised resistance, whereby contingencies are administered by another's conatus instead of one's own, yielding sedimented displeasure that becomes character.
Patience
The determination that the body not move unnecessarily or detrimentally from its place, an active exercise of rational will alongside movement that restores control.
Perfectionism
In its paralyzing form, not excessive demand but a mismatch between the demand for perfection and insufficient attentional resources, producing total inaction.
Phenomenological analysis
An aesthetic method identifying which states of the will a work's sections manifest and how they transition, preceding and grounding technical evaluation of the same work.
Physical movement
A regulatory mechanism whereby sustained bodily activity stabilizes the organism, bodily containment of the lag when external structure has ceased to sustain it.
Pleasure and displeasure
Both are positive magnitudes formed by integrating the other's negative differentials (overcome resistance vs. unattained dominion), correcting Schopenhauer's error.
Plurality of wills
The Code's metaphysical foundation: the organism rests on a concurrence of distinct particular wills, not a single substance, and integration never suppresses that plurality.
Positions of the lamina
The four recurring places — centre, quadrant of deliberation, quadrant of adjudication, and perimeter — that structure the will's deployment at every institutional scale.
Potestative, casual and mixed state
Classifies states by what their occurrence depends on — the organism's own act, foreign forces, or both — with normative consequences for what dispositions can validly command.
Practical reason
Operates on memory, confronting present conditions with falsifiable recollection of past outcomes to vary present action; the foundation of procedural free will.
Presence
The regulating effect certain persons exert on the nervous system merely by being there, independent of the content of the relation, prior to any word.
Procrastination
The postponement of action disguised as planning; list-making functions anxiolytically, reducing anguish over pending matters without the effort of addressing them.
Projection
The mechanism whereby the organism relates to its own idealized image deposited on another, so that recognizing it collapses the motivational energy the bond provided.
Protocols
Self-given norms the organism fixes in favorable states to govern conduct in future states of lesser disposition, a precommitment mechanism favoring systemic solutions.
Purpose
The functional motor that sets the organism in motion, not well-being; when active it sustains action even under distress, functioning as a held direction rather than an enjoyed state.
Rationalization
The mechanism by which intellect justifies, after the fact, thoughts arising affectively; sophistication is not evidence of rationality, and alarm becomes inverted into evidence.
Reciprocity
Not symmetry but the bearing of enduring asymmetries of initiative, need and power; its verifiable form reinforces bonds and generates trust.
Recognition
The valuation others confer that sustains imagined existence; a real but risky dependency, since power belongs to whoever needs others' recognition least.
Recursive generation
The laminae engender one another by the same repeated act, as the Ego meeting another generates the Organization and the technical unit generates the State.
Regime of double version
Distinguishes the Code's binding dispositions from the complementary notes that merely verify them and do not bind interpretation, preserving normative authority over doctrine.
Regulation
The mechanisms — presence, physical movement, interpersonal communication — by which the organism stabilizes itself against dysregulation, arriving from outside the pure volitional act.
Relations of power
Every relation contains a constitutive asymmetry of power, belonging to whoever least needs recognition, without the master-slave dialectic's eventual inversion.
Relief and pleasure (scale)
Not different in kind but in scale: relief is the differential descent of resistance in an instant, pleasure the integral accumulated across a series.
Representational goods
Goods whose value rests on others' opinion rather than utility — personal or institutional honor, office — partitioned by whether a guarantor exists.
Resistance (conatus)
The fundamental concept of the Code: the individual organism's invariant striving against a contrary force, arising from a real plurality of wills, whose accumulation constitutes growth.
Revenue
Goods considered with interval, the differential substance produces over time; revenue requiring presence consumes resistance while civil fruits do not.
Rule
The desire for law willed purely as form, independent of who decides or what custom governs; also generalized memory that keeps habit from mere mechanism.
Rumination
An affective cycle spreading by contiguity rather than logic, generalizing a stimulus toward unrelated existential themes; the Code demands emergence from it rather than argument.
Scale of organization
The criterion of aesthetic evaluation: an element dissonant on one scale may be functional and beautiful on a higher scale that contains it.
Self-binding
A systemic protocol by which the organism binds its present freedom in advance to govern and protect its future, weaker will.
Separation of powers
In the Code's doctrine, a separation of functions (memory legislates, habit executes, liking adjudicates), not of powers, arising from the will's own structure.
Series of ends
The serial structure ordering the organism to successive ends, where determination descends from an ultimate, unknowable end toward proximate ones.
Social layers
The successive strata of the social — family through civil society — each with its own bond and mediation, up to the institutional lamina.
Sources and verification
The regime distinguishing deficient retrospective recollection from independent means of verification such as contemporaneous records, third-party observation, and clinical measurement.
Species of disposition
The four fundamental classes of article — descriptive, predictive, interpretive normative, prescriptive normative — determined by content and governing interpretation and reform.
Standing before (comparecencia)
The relation of standing before the instance as one's measure, a mixed state disposed toward but not commanded or securable by habit.
States of the will
The modifications of the organism's fundamental spherical movement between center and periphery, undergone passively as a continuous affective spectrum.
Statute
The non-negotiated bond proper to public office, where duties are fixed independently of the functionary who occupies the position.
Structural complementary income
Supplementary income treated as a regular structural feature, not as extraordinary, when base income fails to cover regular outflows.
Structures (ternary, binary, serial)
The interpretive doctrine of three structural classes — ternary, binary, and serial — governing how each Book's dispositions are read.
Subjective and objective beauty
A cardinal distinction between subjective beauty (perceived regularity, symmetry, proportion) and objective beauty (fulfilment of an end with grace and adequacy).
Substance and revenue
The two modes of considering goods — substance without temporal interval, revenue with interval — related as integral and differential.
Suffering
The affective correlate of contrary force exerted on the organism, compounded by a reflexive "second sadness" that multiplies rather than adds to it.
Surplus of meaning
The excess of meaning over cause that the ultimate end, though unknowable, lets one perceive obliquely from within the series of ends.
Syncretism
The combination and arrangement of others' materials into a unitary vision, constituting a genuine creative act.
Systemic solutions
Modifying the environment, rather than relying on scarce willpower, as the preferred remedy for recurrent executive deficits; institutionalized as protocols.
Temporal lag
The irreducible interval between desiring something with immediacy and attaining it through time-bound execution; to be tolerated, not suppressed.
Tension of indeterminate kind
A tension whose kind cannot be determined by self-observation while it occurs, recorded as such without retrospective reclassification.
Terminal term of the series
The unknowable end point at which the series of ends terminates, of which nothing — no nature, origin, or identity — may be predicated.
The Administration
Institutionality in act; the given substratum of the State from which legislation, control, and political direction operate.
The beautiful
The foundation of the Code's aesthetics; distinguishes subjective beauty (perceived symmetry and proportion) from objective beauty (fulfilment of an end with grace).
The Code as work
Once the organism dies, the Code ceases to govern and subsists as a contemplable text, itself partaking of the beautiful.
The enclosure
The protective boundary established by the pact, the condition that makes free, voluntary bonding with others possible rather than its negation.
The instance
That before which the organism stands as measure rather than judge or third party, supplying the standard by which the play is judged without judging it itself.
The judge
The one who adjudicates; a party in personal relations absent a third party, but an exterior, non-participating position in institutional adjudication.
The judgment of others
Existence conceived as dependent on being imagined and valued by others, a dependence the Code prescribes emancipating oneself from, especially in work.
The pact
The agreement arising from the insufficiency of spontaneous mediations, establishing the enclosure that protects the weak from the strong.
The State
The highest lamina, identified with its Administration as a given substratum, analogous to how the organism is identified with its character.
The third party
The one who did not play and adjudicates, marking the passage from personal conflict to institutional resolution.
The ugly
An aesthetic category with no autonomous value, justified only when integrated as material into a higher-order beautiful structure that confers it function.
Total vital state
The strategic scale evaluating the whole series of acts by direction toward the fundamental desire, unobservable from within a single act and established only by the archive.
Trait and state
The distinction between a trait, a found property, and a state, a modality of self-relation that comes about rather than being found or willed.
Transitional provisions
Norms of passage that lapse automatically upon fulfillment or extinction of their situation, without express derogation.
Treatment
The prolonged relationship with clinical care, treated as a constitutive datum of the organism rather than a mere biographical accident.
Types of will
Four types of will corresponding to affective zones: naked will to die (sadness), mixed will to die/live (hopelessness, fear), will to dominion (hatred, love), and will to create (joy).
Ultimate motives
The real, underlying ends an activity ultimately seeks, distinct from apparent motives, to be kept in view without attachment.
Validity and reform
The Code's temporal regime: in force from drafting, reformed only expressly, with correction and derogation following the species of disposition.
War of all against all
The baseline condition of relations where, absent a third party, each party judges its own play, generating the necessity of the pact and enclosure.
Will to create
The impulse sustaining the production of new things, oriented to the value created rather than to others' recognition, on pain of degenerating into the will to dominion.
Will to dominion
The most intense mixture of the will to live and the will to die, the affective spring of relations of power between dominator and dominated.
Wisdom
Consolidated knowledge that operates as habit independent of consciousness, though unconsolidated formulations risk being lost.
